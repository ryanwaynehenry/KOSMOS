## HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS
Dennis presents as a new patient with right posterior heel pain radiating up into the calf, typically after workouts and when first getting up in the morning. [2][3]  
He is training for a triathlon with an intensive schedule: runs ~10 km, bikes 45–60 minutes, and swims 30–60 minutes on training days. [4][5][7][9]  
He trains 5 days per week, alternates a long run and long bike on weekends, and takes one day off. [11][13]  
He reports he does not stretch at all and typically “just jump[s] on and go” for biking, though he warms up by starting slow. [14][15][16][17][19]  
Pain is worst on first standing in the morning with tight/tearing sensation, improves after walking ~10–15 minutes, and recurs after prolonged sitting or after a lot of activity. [20][21][25]  
Symptoms are mainly on the right side, with no significant left leg pain. [26][27]  

## PHYSICAL EXAM
Right ankle/foot dorsiflexion (pulling toes up) produces mild pain. [28][29]  
Resisted plantarflexion (pushing against examiner) produces mild pain. [30][31]  
Thompson test is negative. [32][33][36][37][38]  
There is significant tenderness with squeezing the right heel and palpation along the Achilles tendon. [38][39][40][41]  

## RESULTS
Thompson test was negative, which is reassuring against Achilles rupture. [38]  
No imaging was obtained at this visit, as the clinician felt there was no indication for an x-ray at this time. [42]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
Achilles tendinitis, felt to be due to overuse from aggressive triathlon training. [42]  
Recommend stopping training for 1–2 weeks to allow healing before resuming the next phase of training. [42]  
Start ibuprofen/Advil (same medication) 3 tablets every 6 hours as an anti-inflammatory for pain/inflammation. [42]  
Order a couple of physical therapy visits for stretching education and strengthening of the lower extremities. [42]  
Follow up in 7–10 days after the first couple PT appointments to monitor progress. [42][50]  
No x-ray now; if still having significant pain after ~10 days despite activity modification, consider further evaluation and more aggressive treatment. [42]  
Patient agrees with the plan. [43]
