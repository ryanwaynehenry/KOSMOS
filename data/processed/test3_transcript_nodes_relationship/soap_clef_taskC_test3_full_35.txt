## HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS
Matthew presents for evaluation of vision changes, with particular trouble in the right eye. [2]  
He reports difficulty adjusting to dark/dim environments, especially when eating out at night. [3][5]  
Per technician notes reviewed by the clinician, he has decreased vision when reading, especially in dark places. [6]  
He also reports that straight lines (e.g., door frames and the paneling grooves in his office) appear crooked/wavy, which has been present for a while and has worsened over the last 3–4 months. [6][7]  
He currently smokes about 1 pack per day and has had difficulty quitting despite trying hypnosis, lozenges, and chewing gum. [16][17]  
He has a history of high blood pressure and reports he has not taken his blood pressure pill for a while because he has trouble remembering to take it. [18][19]  

## PHYSICAL EXAM
Eye exam/anterior segment exam: posterior chamber intraocular lenses present in both eyes. [22]  
Right eye: cornea clear; no NVI; cup-to-disc ratio 0.2; retina attached 360° with no holes/tears; RPE changes in the fovea; macular drusen with retinal thickening, subretinal fluid, exudate, and hemorrhage. [22]  
Left eye: cornea clear; cup-to-disc ratio 0.3; RPE changes in the fovea; no hemorrhage or subretinal fluid; retina attached appropriately. [22]  

## RESULTS
OCT shows drusen deposits under the RPE and subretinal fluid, with SRF accumulation and a choroidal neovascular membrane visualized. [22]  
Fluorescein angiography shows a focal area of leakage in the right eye consistent with neovascular age-related macular degeneration. [22]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
**1) Neovascular (wet) age-related macular degeneration, right eye, with subretinal fluid and hemorrhage causing visual distortion/decreased vision.** [22]  
The clinician discussed that this is an advanced stage that can lead to more severe vision loss due to abnormal blood vessels growing beneath the retina that leak fluid and blood, potentially causing a central blind spot. [22]  
Plan is intravitreal Lucentis injections once monthly, with the eye numbed beforehand to minimize pain. [22]  
The clinician counseled that injections over time can slow vision decline and possibly stop it, though some vision loss may still occur. [28]  
Patient’s questions/concerns about pain were addressed, and he agreed to proceed as long as it will not hurt a lot. [23][29]  
Nurse will prep the patient and the first injection will be performed today. [32][33]  

**2) Hypertension with medication nonadherence.** [18][19]  
The clinician advised restarting blood pressure medication as prescribed starting today and suggested placing the pill bottle on the counter to help with adherence. [20]  

**3) Tobacco use (1 PPD).** [16][17]  
The clinician advised cutting back or quitting smoking altogether. [18]
