## PHYSICAL EXAM
Cardiac exam: regular rate and rhythm; no murmur appreciated. [72] Back exam: no overlying erythema or ecchymosis. [72] There is midline tenderness at the L4–L5 disc space with right-sided lumbar paravertebral tenderness. [72] Pain is elicited with lumbar flexion and (mildly) with extension. [73][75] Supine straight leg raise is positive. [76][78] Neuro exam: decreased sensation to light touch at the right lateral thigh (L4 dermatome); patellar and Achilles reflexes are symmetric. [78]  

## RESULTS
MRI shows disc desiccation and a disc bulge with paracentral disc herniation resulting in moderate right neural foraminal stenosis. [80] The clinician assessed these findings (with symptoms and exam) as consistent with L4–L5 herniated disc with radiculopathy. [82]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
1) **Chronic low back pain with L4–L5 herniated disc and radiculopathy**: Based on symptoms (radiation/paresthesias), exam (positive straight leg raise and sensory changes), and MRI findings. [17][78][80][82] Plan is to proceed with a fluoroscopy-guided spinal corticosteroid injection to target inflammation and nerve irritation. [82][92] Patient is anxious about needles and requested a pill instead; clinician offered a prescription for an anxiolytic to take about 3 hours before the injection appointment. [83][84] Patient to schedule the injection at the front desk; procedure expected to take about an hour, and if effective would be limited to about four times per year. [103][104]  

2) **Pain control strategy**: Clinician advised that additional pain pills may not be needed after the steroid injection, but if pain persists after about a day post-injection, they will discuss pain medication options. [92][96] Clinician noted that long-term reliance on pain medication is not preferred. [96]  

3) **Escalation if persistent symptoms**: If pain continues despite injection and medication trial, clinician stated surgery may be considered, with intent to try other options first. [96][98]
