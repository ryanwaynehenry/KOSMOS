## HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS
Dennis presents as a new patient with posterior heel pain while training for a triathlon. [2][3]  
He reports pain in the back of the right heel that typically occurs after workouts and is also present when he first gets up in the morning, sometimes radiating up into the calf. [3][20][21][27]  
His training regimen includes running about 10 kilometers, biking 45–60 minutes, and swimming 30–60 minutes, done 5 days per week, with weekend long run/long bike alternation and one rest day. [5][7][9][11][13]  
He reports he does not stretch at all and generally “just jump[s] on and go” for biking, though he warms up by starting slow. [15][16][17][19]  
Morning pain feels tight and “almost like it’s tearing,” improves after walking 10–15 minutes, but returns after prolonged sitting and with lots of activity. [21][25]  

## PHYSICAL EXAM
Pain is mainly on the right side, with no significant left-sided pain reported. [26][27]  
Right ankle/foot maneuvers produced pain with dorsiflexion (pulling toes up) and with resisted pushing against the examiner. [28][29][30][31]  
Thompson test was negative (no pain with calf squeeze and reassuring for no Achilles rupture). [32][33][36][37][38]  
There was significant tenderness with squeezing the right heel and along the Achilles tendon proximally. [38][39][40][41]  

## RESULTS
Thompson test was negative. [38]  
No imaging was obtained at this visit, as the clinician felt there was no indication for an x-ray at this time. [42]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
**Achilles tendinitis (overuse injury) causing right posterior heel/Achilles pain during triathlon training.** [42][27]  
- **Activity modification:** Stop training for 1–2 weeks to allow healing before resuming the next phase of training. [42]  
- **Medication:** Start ibuprofen/Advil 3 tablets every 6 hours as an anti-inflammatory to reduce tendon inflammation and pain. [42]  
- **Physical therapy:** Order a couple PT visits for stretching education and lower-extremity strengthening. [42]  
- **Follow-up:** Return in ~7–10 days after the first couple PT appointments (or within 1 week to 10 days) to monitor progress. [42][50]  
- **Contingency:** If pain persists without improvement despite activity modification (e.g., still significant at ~10 days), consider more aggressive treatment and further evaluation; imaging may be reconsidered then. [42]  
Patient agrees with the plan. [43]
