## HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS
Michelle presents with right knee injury after a skiing fall in Vermont last weekend, when she caught her ski getting off the lift, fell, felt a “pop,” and has had pain since. [1][29]  
She was unable to bear weight and had to be taken down by ski patrol, and she is currently using crutches. [3][24]  
Pain is worst on the outside (lateral) aspect of the knee and she reports a sense of instability/buckling (“won’t hold my weight”). [5][6][7]  
She was able to bend the knee immediately after the injury but now has difficulty bending it, and she can straighten it somewhat but has significant pain with full extension. [9][11]  
She denies numbness/tingling in the toes and thinks toe sensation is intact. [12][13][15]  
She reports being generally healthy and denies a history of high blood pressure or diabetes. [25][26][28]  
At home, she has twins (two 4-year-olds); her husband is currently home but is away often for work, so she is frequently caring for the children herself. [17][19][23]  
For pain control she has been taking ibuprofen with some benefit and declined stronger medication. [33][35][36][37]  
She would like to get back to running but is not currently very active beyond caring for her children. [39][17]  

## PHYSICAL EXAM
Cardiac exam: regular rate and rhythm without murmur. [28]  
Lungs: clear to auscultation. [28]  
Right knee: edema and effusion with pain to palpation over the lateral knee and decreased flexion and extension. [30][31][32]  
Right knee stability testing: positive Lachman sign. [32]  
Neurovascular: dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses palpable, and no ankle/leg edema noted. [32]  

## RESULTS
Right knee x-ray reportedly showed no bony abnormality. [32]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
Right knee injury after skiing fall with effusion, decreased ROM, lateral tenderness, and positive Lachman—concern for right ACL rupture/tear and possible associated structural injury. [1][5][30][32]  
Order MRI of the knee to evaluate for ACL injury and assess for other damaged structures. [32]  
Continue ibuprofen 600–800 mg every 8 hours as needed for pain. [33][38]  
Provide a knee brace for support. [38]  
Plan to obtain MRI within the next couple of days and follow up soon to review results and discuss next steps (including potential surgical planning/graft considerations if ACL injury confirmed). [38][42]
