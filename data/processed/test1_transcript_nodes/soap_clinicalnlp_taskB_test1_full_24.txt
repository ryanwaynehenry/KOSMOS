## HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS
Matthew presents for evaluation of chronic low back pain with worsening over the past month after referral from his PCP. [0][6][7]  
He reports that sitting (including in the waiting room and office chairs) worsens his symptoms, and he has significant functional limitation including difficulty tying his shoes and being “bedridden.” [1][5][27]  
Pain is located in the lower back and radiates down the left hip/leg to the calf and big toe, described as an “electrical toothache.” [9][10][11][23]  
Pain severity is reported as 6/10 currently (off his medications so the clinician could assess him) and up to “13/10” when severe; he also described it as 8/10 with the same radiation pattern. [13][23]  
Pain is worsened by standing, walking, coughing, and sneezing, and improved by lying down. [5][15][17]  
He reports weakness (back feels weak and both legs feel weak), and notes his foot “kind of drags” when he walks and he has been using a cane. [19][23]  
He reports a remote fall from a roof ~30 years ago with possible fracture and longstanding back issues, but states symptoms have been “really bad” for about a month. [7]  
He has tried NSAIDs in the past without relief and has previously done physical therapy. [30]  

## PHYSICAL EXAM
Vitals were reviewed and “look good.” [24]  
Back exam showed no bony abnormalities, redness, or bruising. [24]  
There was tenderness/pain to palpation at the L4 vertebra. [25][26]  
He had pain with lumbar flexion and extension. [28]  
Lower extremity strength was 4/5 on the left and 5/5 on the right. [28][29][30]  
Reflexes were brisk, and motor and sensory exams were intact in both lower extremities. [30]  

## RESULTS
Lumbar spine MRI showed an L4–L5 disc herniation with associated nerve root impingement/compression. [30]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
Assessment: Acute L4–L5 disc herniation with nerve root impingement causing low back pain with left-sided radicular pain. [30]  
Plan: Proceed with epidural steroid injection today to deliver anti-inflammatory medication at the site of inflammation, with counseling that full effect may take 2–3 weeks. [30]  
After injection, refer back to physical therapy to strengthen the area. [30]  
Follow up with the treating clinician in about 1 month. [30]  
Patient agreed to the treatment plan, and the nurse will come in to prep him for the injection. [31][32]
