## HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS
Patient presents for evaluation of worsening headaches after being sent by her PCP. [3][4]  
She reports a long history of headaches since her teenage years, previously associated with menses, and also triggered by stress. [4]  
Recently, the headaches have become more frequent and more severe. [4]  
Headaches are mostly right-sided, described as a severe pulsing sensation located behind the right eye. [5][6]  
She notes headaches worsen with exercise/jogging, limiting how long she can exercise while trying to lose weight. [8]  
Associated symptoms include ringing in the ears for a few months and intermittent blurred vision affecting the side/periphery of her vision. [9][10]  
She denies numbness or tingling of the hands, feet, or face. [11][12]  
She reports feeling nervous about what has been going on with her headaches. [2]  
She has had a prior brain MRI a few years ago, and her PCP planned a more recent MRI but wanted her evaluated urgently first. [21][22]  

## PHYSICAL EXAM
Vitals reviewed by clinician were reassuring, including blood pressure. [23]  
Cardiac exam: normal sinus rhythm without murmurs, rubs, or gallops. [25]  
Eye exam (funduscopic): papilledema noted (swelling behind the eye). [25]  
Neurologic exam: cranial nerves II–XII intact with normal reflexes as described. [25]  
Gait: normal. [25][26]  

## RESULTS
No new imaging or laboratory results were reviewed during this visit; clinician referenced a prior brain MRI from a few years ago. [21]  
Funduscopic exam demonstrated papilledema. [25]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
Worsening chronic headaches with tinnitus and intermittent peripheral visual blurring; exam notable for papilledema, concerning for idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH). [9][10][25][28]  
Discussed that ongoing increased intracranial pressure can lead to permanent vision changes, prompting expedited evaluation. [28]  
Order/obtain brain MRI to evaluate for other contributing conditions; MRI will require insurance preauthorization and then scheduling by the office team. [28][32]  
Plan for lumbar puncture (spinal tap) after MRI to measure intracranial pressure and potentially provide symptomatic relief by removing CSF. [28]  
Refer to neuro-ophthalmology for a complete eye examination. [28]  
Counseled that surgery is not necessarily required for IIH and that medications may be used to lower intracranial pressure; exercise and weight loss are also important parts of treatment. [29][30]
