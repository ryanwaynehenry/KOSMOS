## HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS
The patient presents with persistent right knee pain after falling off a tandem bike with her partner and striking the knee on a curb about 2 weeks ago. [7][8][10][11]  
She reports the knee was normal prior to this injury. [16][17]  
Since the fall, the knee has hurt daily and is “just holding on,” with pain returning after ibuprofen wears off. [11][13]  
She self-treated with ibuprofen, washed the area, and applied a bandage, but symptoms have persisted prompting today’s visit. [11]  
She notes pain is worse with walking uphill/inclines and sometimes feels unstable/shifts when walking, and she sometimes has pain with full extension with a clicking sensation. [57][59][43]  
She works in a children’s hospital rocking babies in the NICU and is usually active with walking her dog; she has not been swimming since the injury. [19][21][77]  

## PHYSICAL EXAM
Right knee exam shows focal medial joint line tenderness, with pes bursa, epicondyle, popliteal space, and lateral side nontender. [38][40][42][43]  
There is a small, dry, dime-sized abrasion over the medial patella (appearing about a week old) and no scars or deformity noted. [43][50]  
Range of motion is full with full extension and symmetric flexion (able to bend all the way back), though the patient reports mild pain/clicking at times with full extension. [43][44][45][50]  
Ligament testing shows MCL and LCL intact, negative anterior drawer and Lachman, and approximately 1+ posterior drawer compared with the other knee. [51][52]  
Patella tracks well with no crepitus, and McMurray testing is negative. [54][56]  

## RESULTS
Right knee x-rays obtained today were read as normal/healthy appearing with no obvious fracture or bone spurs and good-appearing joint space/cartilage and patellar tracking. [84][85][90]  

## ASSESSMENT AND PLAN
Right knee pain after bicycle fall with exam notable for medial joint line tenderness and mild posterior laxity, concerning for possible posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) sprain/injury; meniscal or cartilage injury cannot be excluded. [11][38][52][90][95]  
Order MRI of the right knee to evaluate for internal derangement (PCL vs meniscus/cartilage injury). [92][95]  
Begin nonoperative management with a knee brace (non-custom/temporary) and referral to physical therapy to restore motion and strength, and address swelling. [92][98][110][112]  
Symptom control: continue ice packs a few times per day and may continue ibuprofen short-term (up to three times daily) if helpful; clinician will also prescribe an alternative NSAID dosed twice daily (take with breakfast and dinner) to try first. [98][100][102][104][106]  
Follow up for recheck in 4–6 weeks, with PT frequency individualized but recommended at least a few visits initially to ensure correct exercises and monitor progress. [113][115]  
Discussed that isolated PCL injuries rarely require surgery, but surgery may be considered if persistent instability/symptoms after a period of bracing/therapy and MRI findings warrant. [92][95]
